# Style Guide — Derived from scosman.net blog posts

## Voice

Builder sharing what they made. Not a thought leader arguing a thesis. You're showing your work, not selling a philosophy.

## What to do

- **Start with the thing, not the setup.** No throat-clearing. The SVG post opens with a friend's joke and immediately shows the final animation. The bike rack post opens with a one-line description of what it is. Get to it.
- **Lead with personal context and credentials when relevant.** "I'm an ex-Apple software engineer" — establishes credibility in one clause, then moves on. Don't dwell on it.
- **State the goal plainly.** "The goal: produce code that's as good or better than I would write, without writing any of it." Direct, concrete, no philosophy.
- **Specific numbers, not abstract claims.** "46db" not "too loud." "288 tests" not "comprehensive test coverage." "$48 for 10ft of industrial curtain track" not "affordable materials." Every claim should have a number or a concrete detail behind it.
- **Short paragraphs.** Often just one or two sentences. Let things breathe.
- **Bullet points for specs and lists.** Don't turn a parts list into prose. If it's a list, format it as a list.
- **Self-deprecating honesty over self-promotion.** "Not because I can't, but because I'm lazy" — admitting a real human reason is more convincing than claiming the process is amazing. Let the results speak.
- **Humor lands when it's offhand.** "My only regret: I didn't add an RGB rainbow effect." "aka: Draw the Owl." Never set up a joke — just drop it in and keep going.
- **Acknowledge prior work and alternatives.** "This concept isn't entirely new. SpaceRail is a commercial version of this, but costs $1500." Show you looked around. Don't pretend you invented the wheel.
- **First person, casual.** "I started exploring," "I personally use Figma for this," "I live in a lovely century-old home in Toronto." You're a person talking, not a brand.
- **Parenthetical asides for color.** "(both manual and agentic)" / "(really just a resistor that drops RPM)" — quick tangents that add texture without derailing.
- **Bold for punchlines, key results, or TLDR summaries.** "**TLDR:** The AI wrote every line, but I made the decisions."
- **Assume the reader is competent.** "You'll need to be a bit handy" — don't over-explain. They can figure it out.

## What NOT to do

- **No thesis statements.** "My thesis is simple:" is academic. You don't have a thesis — you have a thing you built and a process that worked.
- **No rhetorical flourish.** "Not to write code, but to think. To make design decisions the AI won't get right on its own." — this is speechwriting, not a blog post.
- **No enterprise pitch language.** "Deliberate process," "production-quality," "human judgment, AI execution" — these read like a product landing page.
- **No defensive positioning.** "This isn't a toy demo" — if you have to say it, show it instead. Specific details (288 tests, full UI test suite) do the convincing.
- **No repeated reframing of the same point.** Say it once, concretely, and move on. Don't restate the thesis in three different ways.
- **No "here's why this matters" framing.** Just show what you did. The reader can decide if it matters.
- **No filler transitions.** "Splitting these into two tools lets you put your best resources where they matter most." — this sentence says nothing. Cut it.
- **No explaining what vibe coding is to the reader.** Don't define it from the outside — just say how your approach relates to it. "It's like vibe coding in that X, but Y" — the reader already knows what vibe coding is.

## Structure patterns from your posts

- **Problem → what I tried → what worked** (fans post)
- **Here's the thing → step by step how to do it** (SVG post, bike rack post)
- **Specific parts/tools/costs → installation/usage → photos** (bike rack)
- Sections are short and scannable. Headers are plain and descriptive.
